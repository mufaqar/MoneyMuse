SCENE 04 Brain psychology — pennies-a-day bias diagram on whiteboard
Voiceover
So why do we do this? It's not about willpower. It's about how your brain processes small numbers. Psychologists call this the "pennies-a-day bias." Your brain sees $4 and thinks "that's nothing." The number is too small to trigger your brain's financial defense system. Companies know this. They're not selling coffee — they're selling a tiny number your brain won't fight. But here's the twist — small spending isn't the enemy. Unconscious spending is. The $5 you consciously choose and enjoy? That's fine. The $5 you spend automatically, habitually, without thinking? That's the leak.

SCENE 05 The leaky boat metaphor — small leak sinks over time
Voiceover
Think of your finances like a boat. One small leak won't sink you today. But if you never patch it, that leak doesn't stay small. It grows. A $5 leak today turns into a $200 leak next year when you add interest, opportunity cost, and the spending habits that expand to fill your income. Most people don't realize they're leaking until they look at their bank statement at the end of the year and wonder where it all went.

SCENE 06 Action plan checklist drawn in marker on whiteboard
Voiceover
Here's the fix. It's not about cutting everything. It's about awareness. Step one: for one week, write down every single thing you spend under $10. Not to judge yourself — just to see. Step two: at the end of the week, look for patterns. What are the "invisible" expenses that add up? And step three: for each pattern, decide consciously — is this something I want to keep, or is this a leak? The goal isn't deprivation. The goal is intention.
